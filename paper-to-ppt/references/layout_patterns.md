# Layout Patterns

Use these archetypes to avoid repetitive slide structures. Pick the layout that best serves the slide's narrative role and available evidence assets.

## Archetypes

1. `hero-thesis`
   - Large thesis statement, small supporting visual, strong opening or transition.
   - Best for title, section, or takeaway slides.

2. `asymmetric-figure-argument`
   - One large paper figure on one side, argument stack or callouts on the other.
   - Best when a single extracted paper figure is the evidence anchor.

3. `figure-zoom-callout`
   - Full or large paper figure with one or two zoomed regions and annotation callouts.
   - Best for dense experimental figures or architecture diagrams.

4. `mechanism-pathway`
   - Multi-step flow or pathway across the slide, with short labels and one anchor figure.
   - Best for methods, pipelines, algorithms, or causal logic.

5. `evidence-matrix`
   - 2x2 or 3-column comparison grid with figures, metrics, or claims.
   - Best for comparing baselines, ablations, datasets, or conditions.

6. `results-dashboard`
   - Several small evidence cards with one dominant result and secondary results.
   - Best for result overview slides.

7. `problem-funnel`
   - Broad context narrows into a precise gap or research question.
   - Best for problem and motivation slides.

8. `before-after-contrast`
   - Two opposing visual zones with a central implication or arrow.
   - Best for showing improvement, difference, or conceptual shift.

9. `layered-architecture`
   - Stacked or nested blocks showing components, interfaces, and information flow.
   - Best for model architecture and system design.

10. `timeline-progression`
   - Horizontal or diagonal progression through stages, experiments, or reasoning steps.
   - Best for procedures, training stages, or paper narrative progression.

11. `evidence-wall`
   - A curated wall of small paper figure crops, each with a tiny label, plus one synthesis statement.
   - Best for showing converging evidence without overexplaining every panel.

12. `discussion-map`
   - Central takeaway with surrounding limitation, implication, and next-step nodes.
   - Best for limitations and closing discussion.

## Selection Rules

- Avoid using the same archetype on adjacent slides.
- For decks with 8 or more slides, use at least five archetypes.
- Keep each slide to 2-4 visual zones.
- Use paper figures as visual anchors whenever possible.
- Use conceptual graphics only when paper figures do not already carry the idea.
- If a slide feels like title-plus-bullets, redesign it with an archetype above.
